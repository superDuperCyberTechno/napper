Napper

A generic role playing game engine

Introduction

Napper is a streamlined, theme-agnostic, d20-based, roll-over, tabletop role-playing game system. In only consists of rules in its rawest form, for specific information on monsters, items, classes etc. refer to an expansion.

Requirements

A complete set of 7 standard polyhedral dice: 1d4, 1d6, 1d8, 1d10, 1d12, 1d20 and 1d100. Pen and paper. That's it.

Checks

When a player character want to achieve any non-trivial action the following happens:

  1. The game master chooses a difficulty (see table below).
  2. The player makes a check against the difficulty, based on the most relevant talent.
Difficulty Value
Very easy 9
Easy 10
Default/normal 11
Hard 12
Very hard 13

To make a check, the player rolls 1d20 and add their talent modifier. If the result is at least as high as the difficulty, the action was a success.

Criticals To determine if a critical event has happened, refer to the below list, ordered from highest, - to lowest priority:
  1. Any check that results in a value of 1 before applying modifier is a critical failure.
  2. Any check that results in a value of 20 before applying modifier is a critical success.
  3. Any check that results in a value of less than 1 after applying modifier is a critical failure.
  4. Any check that results in a value of more than 20 after applying modifier is a critical success.
Powers

Powers represent all supernatural abilities in the game world. In a fantasy setting this would be magic, in a cyberpunk setting it could mean implants allowing super speed, retractable wristblades etc. Just like weapons and other tools, player characters make a talent assignment for each newly acquired power.

Powers are tied to discrete, destructible objects; magic spells is written on scrolls, hacking modules are hardcoded into circuit boards, the void bridge is tied to a mystical totem etc.

Talents

Talents describe the expertise of player characters. Low values being bad, high values being good. When you encounter an action that cannot be represented by any existing talent, make up a new talent that fits and assign it to the player character. - To do so, note your character class' affinity to the talent and make a talent assignment roll:

Roll Handicapped Default Proficient
1-2 -5 -3 -1
3-5 -4 -2 0
6-8 -3 -1 +1
9-12 -2 0 +2
13-15 -1 +1 +3
16-18 0 +2 +4
19-20 +1 +3 +5

Talents (and their level) persist even if a related item should leave a player character's inventory.

Talents vary greatly in theme and specificity. They represent the use of weapon types, the strength to hold a door, health to withstand poison. Pretty much everything that a player character does or uses is expressed using talents: Resist poison, Swords, Two-Handed Swords, Climbing, Dodging, Barter, Threaten, Throw, Pistols, Unarmed Combat, Fireball, X-Ray Vision, Hide, Sneak, Lockpicking, Lie, Invent, Read, etc.

Luck

Sometimes, someone has to draw the shortest straw. Who will the hulking beast attack first? Who will get hit in the head with the falling potted plant? Who will step in the bear trap? Who will literally draw the shortest straw? For this, use Luck.

All player characters start with d6 Luck. If anything unfortunate happens that is not explicitely tied to a specific character, the player characters make a luck roll: 1d20 + luck (reroll on ties). The unfortunate event is inflicted upon the player character with the lowest result who in turn is awarded 1 Luck.

Meta currency

Luck is also a meta currency that can be used by players to enhance their chance of success for checks: 1 luck expended will equal to +1 to their modifier. Expended luck must be resolved prior to rolling.

In addition to talent checks, Luck can also be used in this way to increase the result of talent assignment rolls.

Critical success award

It also acts as a way for game masters to award players characters with 1 Luck if they roll a critical success with no obvious extra benefits to apply.

Advancement

When the game master deems it so (usually after a gruelling encounter) the player characters can try to upgrade a talent they have used. To do so, they make another talent assignment roll (see Talents). If the result is higher than the current level of the talent, they increase the talent level by 1. Alternatively, they can choose to increase their health by 1 without any check.

Combat

Combat is player-facing, meaning that players will make all dice rolls. Prior to combat, players declares what they equip

Armor

Body armor has health of its own. All damage sustained while wearing armor is transferred directly to the armor.

Shields work the same way but must be wielded to have any effect (this takes up one hand). However, unlike body armor, damage sustained is optional.

Some damage explicitely ignore armor and damages the player character health directly.

Reactions

When the game master is uncertain how a non-player character is going to react, they can make a reaction roll using 1d20:

Roll Reaction
1-3 Extreme animosity, will attack
4-6 Angered, very suspicious
7-10 Dismissive, prefer to be left alone
11-14 Interested, wants more information
15-17 Friendly, will help without much incentive
18-20 Elated, will do a lot and ask few questions

The game master can apply modifiers to the reaction roll if there are circumstances that could have an impact on it.

Inventory

Player characters can carry 10 items. Of these items, characters can carry 1 huge item. Player characters can carry infinite tiny items.
